Creative Coding – Final Assessment Brief
 Objective

This assignment is designed to test your knowledge and skills acquired throughout the semester. You will be working in teams of 3–4 to create an interactive visual work using p5.js. Every team member will act as the creative director for one mechanic. A mechanic is a distinct interaction mode that drives how your piece behaves. Each team member will be responsible for their chosen mechanic’s design, development, and implementation.

 

The Task

As a team, choose one of the following two options:

 

Option 1 – Reinterpret an existing artwork: Choose an existing artwork and write code to represent it (whether by copying, abstracting, or through another representative mode), then extend it with interactive mechanics using p5.js. Before you write your code, think about the characteristics of the artwork: Is it defined by distinct lines, simple/flat partitions, and structural design, or does it have more complex or layered geometric patterns? How would you choose to interpret or represent it? Consider the tools and techniques you've learned so far and how they might be applied to capture the essence of the artwork. Be creative with how your team interprets the work. It does not have to be a perfect copy. Well-thought-out, well-researched creative interpretations are better. You must receive approval from your tutor if you plan to go this route through the Quizzes week 8 and 9 (see below).

Option 2 – Create an original piece: Design and build your own interactive visual work from scratch. Use the inspiration sections of the weekly tutorials as inspiration to explore what kind of piece your team wants to create.

 

Mechanics

Each team member must serve as the creative director for one mechanic. Each member must choose a different mechanic from the following:

Audio: Use the level or frequency content of an audio track to drive your mechanic.
Time-based: Employ timers and events to drive your mechanic.
Perlin noise and randomness: Utilise Perlin noise AND random values or random seed to drive your mechanic.
User input: Incorporate mouse or keyboard inputs to drive your mechanic.
 

No two members in the same team may choose the same mechanic.

 

Code Structure

Your project must be modular. Each mechanic must live in its own separate script file, named descriptively (e.g., audio-mechanic.js, perlin-layer.js, input-controls.js). This keeps your code readable, makes individual contributions clear, and prevents overly long sketch files. Your main sketch.js should bring these scripts together.

 

 

Milestones

In Week 8, you will complete a quiz where you individually reflect on an inspiration and the mechanic you would like to develop. In Week 9, your team will submit a short proposal describing what you plan to build and which mechanic each member will develop.

These quizzes are marked as part of your regular quiz grades, but they are designed to directly support your final project. Use them as an opportunity to get feedback from your tutor and to discuss whether your project idea is on the right track before you commit to it. Taking these seriously will save you significant time later.

Week 13 Lecture and Tutorial will act as consultation time. Your attendance will be based on your team having a one-to-one discussion with your tutor about the project's progress. Any questions, any issues, or anything that might arise from the development of your project.

 

Deliverables

GitHub Submission

Each team must submit its work via a GitHub repository. The repository must contain:

 Code: A functional prototype of your project containing all the necessary files to run it. It must be made using p5.js, with each mechanic in its own separate script file. The repository must have at least three meaningful commits per team member (excluding initial project setup), and the commit history must clearly show each member's individual contributions.

 README.md: Your repository must include a well-formatted README.md file containing:

Inspiration: What inspired your team's project? This might include artworks, websites, games, concepts, or specific aspects of the source material. Explain briefly how these inspirations shaped your approach.
Techniques: What p5.js techniques were implemented in the project and why? Give a short overview of how your code works and the key decisions your team made.
Mechanic ownership: Which team member was responsible for which mechanic, and a brief description of what each mechanic does.
AI acknowledgement: If you used ChatGPT, Claude, DeepSeek, or any other AI tool to help generate code, you must reference it here and explain what it was used for and how the generated code works. This must also be commented in the actual code (e.g. // this code was generated with the help of ChatGPT and does xyz).
External references: If you borrowed or were influenced by code found online, in a book, or from any other source, reference it with a link and an explanation. This must also be commented in the code (e.g. // this technique is from www.source-url.com).
Interaction instructions: How does someone experience your work? E.g., "move the mouse slowly over the screen," "click play and wait for audio to start," "use arrow keys to move."
 

Code comments: Your code should be well commented throughout. Use clear variable names so it is easy to understand what your code is doing. For any tools or techniques used outside the course, add a short comment explaining what they do and where they came from.

Tips

Plan your approach as a team but be prepared for things to change. What you choose to do during weeks 8 and 9 is not set in stone. There will be room to change.
Divide responsibilities clearly. Each member owns their mechanic from start to finish.
You are encouraged to apply all techniques learned in class.
Modular code will earn you higher marks. Use functions and classes to keep your code readable.
Ensure your project scales correctly when the browser window is resized.
Use clear variable names and comment your code well.
If you incorporate something not covered in class, always reference the source and explain how it works.
Be creative with how your team interprets the brief. Surprising and aesthetically powerful outcomes are great.
Take the Week 8 and 9 milestone quizzes seriously. They are your best chance to get early feedback.
